## Item 1 — EU Digital Omnibus on AI: High-Risk Deadline Deferral Pending Formal Adoption

### Part A — News item summary

1. **Title:** Digital Omnibus on AI — provisional agreement deferring high-risk obligations (amending Regulation (EU) 2024/1689)
2. **Source:** European Parliament / Council of the EU (Council doc 9247/26, endorsing 9034/26); reported by White & Case, Gibson Dunn, Inside Privacy, Travers Smith, DLA Piper
3. **Publication date:** Provisional political agreement 6–7 May 2026; Council compromise text 13 May 2026; as of mid-June 2026 still pending formal adoption (Parliament plenary vote expected in June, OJ publication expected ~late July)
4. **Link:** https://www.gibsondunn.com/eu-ai-act-omnibus-agreement-postponed-high-risk-deadlines-and-other-key-changes/
5. **Jurisdiction / institution:** European Union / co-legislators (Parliament + Council)
6. **One-sentence factual summary:** The Digital Omnibus on AI would defer standalone Annex III high-risk obligations from 2 August 2026 to 2 December 2027 (and Annex I embedded-product systems to 2 August 2028), add a new Article 5 prohibition on AI-generated non-consensual intimate imagery and CSAM, and introduce targeted simplifications — but as of this run it is not yet law, so the original 2 August 2026 high-risk date remains the binding legal text until publication in the Official Journal.
7. **Why selected:** The deferral is explicitly driven by the **non-availability of harmonised standards and compliance tools** (CEN/CENELEC mandate unmet) — i.e., the high-risk regime cannot enter application because the conformity-assessment and admissibility machinery does not yet exist. This is a governance-architecture signal: the regulation's authorization/admissibility layer is being held open because the structural instruments to operationalize it are absent. ABCF is not mentioned in the source; relevance is architectural. Matched concepts: execution/admissibility boundaries (conformity assessment as the gate before high-risk deployment is permitted); the gap between nominal regulatory authority and the structural capacity to exercise it.
8. **Relevant ABCF components:** Execution/admissibility boundaries; conformity assessment as output gating; institutional authorization of AI use; rooted authorization (CE marking + Declaration of Conformity as the authorization act)
9. **Relevance level:** Direct relevance (policy and architecture)

### Part B — ABCF lens analysis

- **ABCF coherence:** Moderate to High
- **ABCF alignment:** Background-to-aligned (policy-relevant; not a structural validation)
- **Infringement or tension:** None
- **Structural analysis:** The Omnibus is coherent with ABCF as a *negative* signal — it demonstrates that an admissibility regime is only as real as the instruments that operationalize it. The EU built the authorization architecture (conformity assessment, CE marking, presumption of conformity via harmonised standards) but cannot switch it on because the standards layer is incomplete. ABCF's position that "interpretation must not become authority" has a procedural analogue here: a regulation in force is not the same as a regulation that can be complied with or enforced. The deferral does not close the structural accountability gap ABCF identifies; it only moves the clock.
- **Known facts:** Provisional agreement reached 6–7 May 2026; Council compromise text (9247/26) published 13 May 2026; deferral to 2 Dec 2027 (Annex III) and 2 Aug 2028 (Annex I); new Article 5 prohibition on AI NCII/CSAM; not yet adopted; 2 Aug 2026 remains the legal date pending OJ publication.
- **Inferences:** Formal adoption before 2 Aug 2026 is widely expected but not guaranteed. If the trilogue/plenary process slips, the original deadline applies as written.
- **Unknowns:** Final adopted text may differ from the compromise; legal-linguistic revision pending; downstream effect on CEN/CENELEC standards timeline not specified.
- **ABCF classification:** Coherent but incomplete

## Item 2 — In re: ChatGPT Product Liability Cases (JCCP No. 5431): Product-vs-Service as Dispositive

### Part A — News item summary

1. **Title:** In re: ChatGPT Product Liability Cases, JCCP No. 5431 (coordinated proceeding) — and parallel Garcia v. Character Technologies product characterization
2. **Source:** California Superior Court, San Francisco County; reported by National Law Review, Bloomberg Law, Moody's
3. **Publication date:** Coordination order February 2026; commentary through late May–June 2026
4. **Link:** https://natlawreview.com/article/ai-reckoning-has-arrived-case-will-rewrite-ai-laws-products-liability
5. **Jurisdiction / institution:** California / Superior Court (coordinated JCCP); cf. M.D. Fla. (Garcia)
6. **One-sentence factual summary:** Twelve product-liability cases against OpenAI were coordinated in California in February 2026, and the threshold legal question — whether a generative AI system is a "product" (subject to strict product liability for defective design) or a "software-based service" — is shaping up as dispositive, following Garcia v. Character Technologies, where the court treated the chatbot as a product because the claims targeted specific design features.
7. **Why selected:** The product-vs-service determination is a **judicial authority-tracing exercise**: it asks whether the deploying entity controls the generative function as a designed artifact (product → liability attaches to the controller) or merely transmits a service. This is structurally the same move as the Munich AI Overviews ruling (Runs 3–4) — locating responsibility by identifying who holds substantive control over the generative mechanism. `[PROXIMITY SIGNAL — RJB PUBLICATION: P5]` Matched concepts: output authorship/liability as a function of identifiable control over the generative function; the containment boundary between "service transmission" and "authored product"; admissibility of AI output as a litigable design question rather than a presumption.
8. **Relevant ABCF components:** Containment boundaries; execution/admissibility boundaries; causal attribution of AI-generated content; rooted authorization (control = author = liable party); human oversight failures (design-defect theory)
9. **Relevance level:** Direct relevance

### Part B — ABCF lens analysis

- **ABCF coherence:** High
- **ABCF alignment:** Partial to Strong (problem signal from the field; convergent with P5 attribution structure)
- **Infringement or tension:** Low (independent field development, not infringement of RJB)
- **Structural analysis:** The litigation forces courts to resolve an attribution question by classifying the *nature* of the generative artifact. ABCF's claim that admissibility cannot be presumed — that AI output must pass an explicit check before it is treated as authored, relied upon, or actionable — appears here as the live question of whether strict liability (product) or fault-based liability (service) governs. The defendants' own framing ("software-based service") is an attempt to keep the output on the service side of the containment boundary; the plaintiffs' design-defect theory pulls it to the product side. Either way, the court is tracing authority through the control chain, which is the SLT/P5 structure.
- **Known facts:** 12 cases coordinated Feb 2026 (JCCP 5431); Garcia treated chatbot as product at motion-to-dismiss stage; OpenAI characterizes ChatGPT as a service; classification not yet finally resolved in the coordinated proceeding.
- **Inferences:** A product characterization could weaken Section 230 defenses and generalize design-defect exposure across deployers. The "tobacco/asbestos playbook" framing suggests mass-tort consolidation dynamics.
- **Unknowns:** How the coordinated proceeding will rule on product-vs-service; whether appellate courts will harmonize the split implied by conflicting district rulings.
- **ABCF classification:** Coherent problem signal

## Item 3 — NIST CAISI AI Agent Standards Initiative: Identity and Authorization as a Standards Target

### Part A — News item summary

1. **Title:** NIST CAISI AI Agent Standards Initiative (RFI NIST-2025-0035; AI Agent Interoperability Profile planned Q4 2026; COSAiS SP 800-53 AI overlays)
2. **Source:** NIST / Center for AI Standards and Innovation (CAISI); reported by Pillsbury, Cloud Security Alliance Lab Space
3. **Publication date:** Initiative announced 17 February 2026; RFI responses March–April 2026; profile planned Q4 2026
4. **Link:** https://www.pillsburylaw.com/en/news-and-insights/nist-ai-agent-standards.html
5. **Jurisdiction / institution:** United States / NIST CAISI (within ITL)
6. **One-sentence factual summary:** CAISI launched a voluntary standards effort for autonomous AI agents whose three named focus areas are identity and authorization, security and risk management, and monitoring and logging, with an AI Agent Interoperability Profile planned for Q4 2026 and a forthcoming SP 800-53 extension (COSAiS) providing single- and multi-agent control overlays.
7. **Standards status:** Draft / under development (RFI stage; profile and overlays forthcoming)
8. **Why selected:** "Identity and authorization" as a first-order standards axis for autonomous agents is a direct operationalization of **rooted human authorization** — the requirement that an agent's authority originate from an identifiable, authenticated source rather than being self-generated. The RFI drew responses from the OpenID Foundation and a financial-services coalition (BPI, ABA, BITS), indicating institutional convergence on identity federation and explicit delegation as the governance primitives for agentic AI. ABCF is not mentioned in the source; relevance is architectural. `[PROXIMITY SIGNAL — RJB PUBLICATION: P1, P3]` Matched concepts: rooted human authorization; authorization topology and delegation; the named-integrator / anchor-loss problem in multi-agent chains (P3 / TR-SA-1.1).
9. **Relevant ABCF components:** Rooted human authorization; non-delegation of authority to AI; authorization topology; runtime monitoring and logging; containment boundaries

### Part B — ABCF lens analysis

- **ABCF coherence:** High
- **ABCF alignment:** Strong (architectural convergence on authorization as a governance primitive)
- **Infringement or tension:** Low (independent standards work; convergent, not derivative)
- **Structural analysis:** CAISI is building, at the standards layer, the same authorization-rooting and delegation-tracing structure ABCF specifies at the architectural layer. The emphasis on agent identity, short-lived authenticated tokens, trusted-agent registries, and prohibition of cross-agent privilege delegation absent explicit authorization maps closely onto ABCF's rooted-authorization and non-delegation principles. The structural gap ABCF still holds: CAISI's framing is security-and-interoperability-first (who is the agent, can it be trusted, is the call authenticated), whereas ABCF's claim is about *identifiability of causal contribution* under endogenous adaptive use — a layer that authentication and logging do not reach. Authentication establishes provenance of the call, not identifiability of the causal share of the outcome.
- **Known facts:** Initiative announced 17 Feb 2026; three focus areas (identity/authorization, security/risk, monitoring/logging); profile planned Q4 2026; COSAiS overlays forthcoming; OpenID Foundation and financial-sector coalition submitted formal responses.
- **Inferences:** Identity/authorization standards may flow into FedRAMP AI requirements via COSAiS, giving the authorization-rooting principle procurement-level force.
- **Unknowns:** Whether the profile will address causal identifiability/attribution at all, or remain at the authentication/provenance layer.
- **ABCF classification:** Coherent and aligned

2. **Source:** ISO/IEC JTC 1/SC 42; secondary: Pacific Certifications, SGS, AI Safety Directory
3. **Publication date:** Published 2025 (now in catalogue alongside ISO/IEC 42005:2025); active 2026
4. **Link:** https://www.iso.org/standard/42006
5. **Jurisdiction / institution:** ISO/IEC (international)
6. **One-sentence factual summary:** ISO/IEC 42006:2025 sets the additional competence and consistency requirements (building on ISO/IEC 17021-1) for certification bodies that audit and certify AI management systems against ISO/IEC 42001, emphasizing auditor competence in AI model lifecycle, data risk, transparency and traceability evidence, and consistent grading of AI-governance nonconformities.
7. **Standards status:** Published
8. **Why selected:** 42006 operationalizes the **admissibility/assurance layer**: it defines what it takes for an external body to be competent to certify that an organization's AI governance is real, including review of "transparency and traceability evidence tied to AI decisions." This is the conformity-assessment machinery whose absence is delaying the EU high-risk regime (Item 1). ABCF is not mentioned in the source; relevance is architectural. Matched concepts: audit/assurance as an admissibility gate; traceability evidence as the object of certification; the competence boundary required before certification authority is exercised.
9. **Relevant ABCF components:** Execution/admissibility boundaries; auditability and assurance; AI decision provenance and traceability; institutional authorization (certification as a rooted authorization act)

### Part B — ABCF lens analysis

- **ABCF coherence:** Moderate to High
- **ABCF alignment:** Aligned (assurance-layer convergence)
- **Infringement or tension:** None
- **Structural analysis:** 42006 is coherent with ABCF's admissibility logic: certification authority should not be exercised by bodies lacking the competence to assess AI-specific traceability and risk. It builds the institutional check that stands between "we have an AI management system" and "an authorized body confirms it." The structural gap: certification confirms the *existence and operation* of a management system; it does not resolve, and does not claim to resolve, the causal-identifiability question ABCF isolates. Traceability evidence ≠ structural identifiability of causal contribution.
- **Known facts:** 42006:2025 published; builds on ISO/IEC 17021-1; targets certification-body competence for ISO/IEC 42001 audits; 42005:2025 (impact assessment) also published.
- **Inferences:** With 42006 in place, third-party 42001 certification becomes more credible and consistent, which may feed EU Article 17 (QMS) compliance pathways once harmonised standards land.
- **Unknowns:** Adoption rate among accreditation bodies; how rigorously "traceability evidence" will be assessed in practice.
- **ABCF classification:** Coherent and aligned
