### Item 1 — NIST AI Agent Standards Initiative + ITL Agent Identity and Authorization Concept Paper

**PART A**

1. **Title:** AI Agent Standards Initiative (CAISI) + AI Agent Identity and Authorization Concept Paper (ITL / NCCoE)
2. **Source:** NIST (nist.gov primary announcement); NCCoE concept paper
3. **Publication date:** Initiative launched 2026-02-17; RFI on AI Agent Security due 2026-03-09; ITL Identity & Authorization Concept Paper comment due 2026-04-02
4. **Link:** https://www.nist.gov/news-events/news/2026/02/announcing-ai-agent-standards-initiative-interoperable-and-secure
5. **Jurisdiction / institution:** NIST (US federal), Center for AI Standards and Innovation (CAISI), Information Technology Laboratory (ITL), National Cybersecurity Center of Excellence (NCCoE)
6. **One-sentence factual summary:** A US federal standards initiative for autonomous AI agents organized around three areas — agent identity and authentication, action logging and auditability, and containment boundaries for autonomous operation — with a concept paper treating agents as identifiable entities requiring explicit identification, authentication, and per-action authorization.
7. **Why selected:** §3a standards vector. Most concrete standards-body movement toward ABCF-component territory observed to date. `[PROXIMITY SIGNAL — RJB PUBLICATION: P1, P2, P3]` — convergent on **containment boundaries**, **rooted authorization / named-integrator topology** (agents as identifiable entities, not anonymous shared-credential automation; per-action authorization scoping), and **execution-gate / admissibility** machinery (human approval gates for sensitive actions, non-repudiation logging).
8. **Relevant ABCF component(s):** containment boundaries; explicit human authorization; authorization acts; named accountable human integrators; execution gates; admissibility checks; model audit trails; decision traceability.
9. **Field 10 — Standards status:** Under development (RFIs / concept paper / listening sessions phase; COSAiS SP 800-53 control overlays for single- and multi-agent systems in development; no published standard yet).
10. **Relevance level:** Direct relevance.

**PART B**

- **Coherence:** High. The three named pillars map almost one-to-one onto ABCF's containment-boundary, rooted-authorization, and admissibility/audit components. "Treat agents as identifiable entities rather than anonymous automation under shared credentials" is the operational shadow of the named-integrator requirement.
- **Alignment:** Strongly aligned in direction. Per-action authorization scoping, human approval gates for sensitive actions, and containment boundaries for autonomous operation are ABCF execution/admissibility-boundary instantiations at the standards layer.
- **Infringement / tension:** None. The principal gap is framing: this is **identity/security-rooted**, not **identifiability-rooted**. Authorization here means credential/permission scoping, not the geometric identifiability precondition (substantive vs. nominal authority) that ABCF P3 requires. The standard could harden into authority-leakage exposure if "agent authenticated" is later treated as "agent's causal contribution attributable."
- **Known facts:** Initiative launched 2026-02-17 by CAISI; three areas (identity/authentication, action logging/auditability, containment boundaries); NCCoE concept paper proposes treating agents as identifiable entities with per-action authorization; multiple industry comments filed (incl. recommendations to extend OAuth 2.0, NIST SP 800-63, SPIFFE/SPIRE, W3C VC).
- **Inferences:** This is a candidate future home for an ABCF-shaped requirement — if identity/authorization standards add an identifiability precondition, they would operationalize rooted authorization in a way ABCF formalizes. Conversely, if they stop at credential identity, they instantiate the nominal-authority case P3 names as insufficient. *(Inference — NIST does not reference ABCF/RJB; relevance architectural.)*
- **Unknowns:** Whether forthcoming guidance introduces any causal-identifiability or substantive-authority condition; final standard form and date.
- **ABCF classification:** Coherent but incomplete.

### Item 2 — The Answerability Fuse: A Statutory Trigger for Reconstructable Decisions

**PART A**

1. **Title:** The Answerability Fuse: A Statutory Trigger for Reconstructable Decisions
2. **Source:** Zenodo / ResearchGate (working paper)
3. **Publication date:** 2026-06 (DOI dated; accessed reference 2026-06-09)
4. **Link:** https://doi.org/10.5281/zenodo.20631111
5. **Jurisdiction / institution:** Working paper (legal-theoretic; not stated)
6. **One-sentence factual summary:** Argues that the prior governance question is not who absorbs responsibility when attribution fails (proxy responsibility) but whether the original decision can still be *reconstructed and reopened at all*, and proposes a statutory trigger whose duties run to preserving that reconstructability.
7. **Why selected:** §3 accountability / decision-provenance theme. Distinguishes a *prior* question (reconstructability) from downstream responsibility allocation — structurally parallel to ABCF's attribution-prior-condition logic and the liability-sink critique. `[PROXIMITY SIGNAL — RJB PUBLICATION: P5]` — convergent on the **liability-sink** concept (a proxy can absorb responsibility while the decision remains as unreconstructable as before) and on attribution/reconstructability as a precondition rather than a post-hoc allocation.
8. **Relevant ABCF component(s):** attribution indeterminacy; accountability gaps; decision traceability; provenance chains.
9. **Relevance level:** Indirect relevance.

**PART B**

- **Coherence:** High. The "reconstruct and reopen" framing is a provenance/traceability precondition; the explicit critique of proxy responsibility as leaving the decision "exactly as unreconstructable as before" is the liability-sink failure mode P5 names.
- **Alignment:** Aligned. Treats reconstructability as the thing to preserve rather than assuming attribution is recoverable — directionally compatible with ABCF's attribution-prior posture.
- **Infringement / tension:** None. It does not reach the *structural* claim (that reconstructability can be ontologically, not just practically, unavailable above a threshold); it frames the problem as statutory-design, not structural-limit.
- **Known facts:** Proposes a statutory trigger ("fuse") whose duties preserve reconstruction/reopening capacity; explicitly distinguishes from proxy responsibility.
- **Inferences:** Stops short of SLT — treats reconstructability as preservable by statutory design, where P1/P5 would ask whether the admissible observation regime can resolve it at all. A near-neighbor that has not crossed into structural indeterminacy. *(Inference.)*
- **Unknowns:** Author identity/affiliation; whether later versions engage structural non-identifiability; venue.
- **ABCF classification:** Coherent but incomplete.
